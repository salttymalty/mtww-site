How to Choose a Musical for Your School or Youth Program

Musical Theatre Worldwide

Choosing a musical for your school or youth theater program is one of the most important decisions you'll make each season. The right show energizes your cast, engages your audience, and fits your program's capabilities. The wrong one leads to frustrated kids, overworked directors, and underwhelming performances.

Here's a practical framework for making the right choice, drawn from 30 years of producing youth musicals at The Actors Garden in Oak Park, Illinois.

Start with Your Cast, Not Your Wish List

The most common mistake: picking a show you love and then trying to force-fit your students into it. Instead, start by honestly assessing who will be in your cast. How many students? What age range? What's their experience level? A show with 8 roles won't work for a program with 30 kids. A show written for high schoolers won't land with 3rd graders, no matter how much you simplify the language.

Look for shows where the cast size matches your enrollment. Shows with flexible chorus roles are especially valuable — they let you include every student without inventing extra parts that dilute the story.

Written For vs. Adapted For

There's a meaningful difference between musicals originally written for young performers and 'Junior' versions of adult shows. Junior adaptations cut material to shorten runtime and simplify content, but the roles were still conceived for adult actors. The comedy, the emotional beats, the vocal demands — they were designed for grown-ups.

Musicals written specifically for young performers build from the ground up with kids in mind. The humor works for their age. The vocal ranges fit their voices. The themes resonate with their lives. The roles are designed to challenge them at their level, not present a watered-down version of an adult experience.

The Five Questions That Matter

Before you commit to a show, answer these honestly:

1. Can my students sing this music? Listen to the score. Are the ranges reasonable for your cast? Does the music require trained singers or can enthusiastic beginners handle it? Shows with ensemble-heavy numbers are more forgiving than those built around demanding solos.

2. Does the content fit my community? Every school and program has its own comfort zone. Read the full script, not just the synopsis. Make sure the themes, language, and subject matter work for your students, their families, and your administration.

3. Can I stage this with my resources? Some shows need elaborate sets and costumes. Others can be performed with minimal staging. Be realistic about your budget, your space, and your volunteer base. A simple show done well always outperforms an ambitious show done poorly.

4. Will every student have something meaningful to do? The best youth theater experiences give every child a moment in the spotlight — a line, a song, a featured bit. Look for shows that distribute stage time generously, not ones that spotlight three leads while the ensemble stands in the background.

5. Does this show teach something? Not a lesson — theater isn't school. But the best youth musicals help kids develop empathy, collaboration, timing, and confidence. Look for shows where the rehearsal process itself is valuable, not just the final performance.

What to Look For in a Licensing Package

Once you've narrowed your options, compare what each publisher includes in their licensing fee. At minimum, you should receive: a printable script, a piano/vocal score, vocal lead sheets, and backing tracks. Some publishers charge per-script rental fees or require you to return materials — this adds cost and hassle. Others, like Musical Theatre Worldwide, include everything in a single flat fee with no rental charges.

Always request a perusal copy before committing. A good publisher will let you read the full script before you pay for the license. If they won't, that's a red flag.

Start Small, Learn, Repeat

If you're launching a new program or working with young students for the first time, start with a shorter show (30-45 minutes). Your students will build confidence, your team will learn the production process, and you'll have a success to build on. You can always go bigger next season.
